James Scott gives a foray into a somewhat speculative agriculture of pre-history of Europe and West Asia. The driving premise is that the 'pre-historical' agriculture was fairly ecologically stable and widely practiced at the same time as historical agricultural systems. Because the institutions of cities and early states allowed for maintenance of records, our knowledge of historical agricultural systems is biased to preserving agricultural methods which supported cities and states.

At the center of these state agricultural systems is the ability to easily assess and collect taxes. This favors goods which are hard to hide, ripens at near uniform times, easily harvested (or destroyed), and easily transported. This gives to a favoring of grains in cities and states, confirmed by comparison of dental records from those within and outside of state control and other geologic and archeological records.
